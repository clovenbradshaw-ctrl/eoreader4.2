Gregor Samsa woke one morning from uneasy dreams to find himself transformed in his bed into a monstrous vermin.
He lay on his armour-hard back and saw his many thin legs waving helplessly before his eyes.
Gregor was a travelling salesman, and the case of samples still stood beside his bed.
A picture hung on the wall above the table where Gregor had sat to read.
The alarm clock showed that Gregor had already overslept the early train.
His mother knocked gently on the locked door and called his name in a worried voice.
The chief clerk arrived at the flat to ask why Gregor had missed the train.
Gregor tried to answer, but only a strange twittering noise came from behind the door.
When the door finally opened the chief clerk fled down the stairs in horror.
His father drove Gregor back into the room with a heavy walking stick.
Gregor crawled across the floor and hid beneath the couch where the darkness comforted him.
His sister Grete brought a bowl of milk, the drink Gregor had loved before the change.
Grete set the food down quietly and watched the bowl from the doorway.
Gregor turned away from the fresh milk, for the old tastes disgusted him now.
The next morning Grete brought scraps and rotten vegetables, and these he ate eagerly.
Grete alone dared to enter the room, and she fed Gregor every day.
Gregor's sister cleaned the floor and learned which leftovers her brother preferred.
Grete opened the window each morning so that Gregor could breathe the cool air.
She decided to remove the furniture so that her brother could crawl freely over the walls.
The mother protested, fearing that clearing the room would abandon hope of Gregor's recovery.
Grete and the mother carried the heavy chest of drawers out of the room together.
Gregor clung to the picture on the wall to save one possession from being taken.
His mother saw Gregor on the wall and fainted in terror at the sight.
The father returned and hurled apples at Gregor, and one apple lodged in his back.
The wound festered for weeks, and Gregor could no longer move as he once had.
The family took in three lodgers to make money, and the flat grew crowded and tense.
One evening Grete played the violin for the lodgers in the living room.
Gregor crept out to hear the music, drawn by the only beauty left to him.
A lodger spotted the vermin in the doorway and angrily gave notice to leave.
The father apologised to the lodgers while the mother wept into her hands.
Grete turned to her parents and said the creature must go before it ruined them all.
She declared that the family could not keep torturing themselves by calling the thing Gregor.
The sister insisted they had tried everything humanly possible to bear their burden.
Grete said they must get rid of it, for keeping it would destroy the whole family.
The father agreed that the lodger, the wound, and the burden had broken their patience at last.
Gregor retreated to his room for the final time, weak and resigned to their wish.
He thought of his family with tenderness even as his breath grew shallow.
Gregor died quietly before dawn, and the charwoman found his dry husk in the morning.
The family felt a great relief and took a tram into the country to plan a new life.
Grete had grown into a healthy young woman, and her parents thought of finding her a husband.

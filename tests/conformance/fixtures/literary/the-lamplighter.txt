THE LAMPLIGHTER'S ROUND

Aldric had walked the lamplighter's round for eleven years, and in all that time he had never once been late. Every evening, as the sky over the harbor turned the color of a bruise, he would take up his long brass pole and begin at the corner of Anchor Street, working his way inland toward the old chapel.

On the evening this story concerns, a stranger was waiting for him at the third lamp. The stranger wore a coat too heavy for the season and stood so still that Aldric nearly walked past without seeing him. When their eyes met, the stranger spoke first.

"You light these every night," the stranger said. It was not a question.

Aldric admitted that he did. He asked the stranger what business he had on Anchor Street at that hour, but the stranger only smiled and asked whether Aldric had ever wondered who put out the lamps he lit — for surely, the stranger said, someone must, each morning, before the sun made the work invisible.

Aldric had never wondered this. He said so.

The stranger nodded slowly, as though this answer confirmed something he had long suspected, and walked on ahead of Aldric toward the chapel, leaving footprints in the wet cobblestones that Aldric found, when he looked again, were already gone.

At the chapel steps sat an old woman who sold violets by day and, it was said, told fortunes by night for those who asked politely. She had known Aldric's mother, and she called out to him now as she always did.

"Walking with company tonight?" the old woman asked, nodding toward the place where the stranger had been standing a moment before. But when Aldric turned, the stranger was gone.

He told the old woman what had passed between them — the question about the lamps, the smile, the vanishing. She listened without interrupting, her hands folded over her basket of violets.

"He asked you who puts them out," she said, when Aldric had finished. "Not who lights them. He already knew that part." She said this as though it settled something, though Aldric could not have said what.

Aldric asked her if she knew the stranger. She said that she and her sister had seen him before, years ago, though her sister no longer believed the story and would not thank Aldric for repeating it. The two women had argued about the stranger every winter since, she said, and neither had persuaded the other.

Aldric finished his round that night the way he finished every round: lamp by lamp, working inland from the harbor. But he found himself watching the shadows between the lamps more closely than he had before, and when he reached the last lamp on Cobbler's Lane, he thought — though he could not be certain — that he saw the stranger again, standing just beyond the light, watching him work.

He did not speak to the stranger a second time. He only nodded, the way one neighbor nods to another across a street too wide for conversation, and the stranger nodded back.

The next morning, walking to the harbor to begin his day, Aldric passed the old woman setting out her violets. She asked whether he had seen the stranger again, and he told her that he had, though nothing further had passed between them.

"That's how it goes with him," she said. "A little more each time, until one night he tells you the rest."

Aldric asked what the rest was.

The old woman only shook her head and began arranging her violets, and Aldric — though he waited a moment longer than politeness required — understood that she would say no more that morning.
